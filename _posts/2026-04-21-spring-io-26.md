Spring I/O is something I discovered on YouTube a few years ago and have been a huge fan of ever since. I used to watch each and every published video and stay amazed by the quality and the general sense of community. Year after year I looked forward to seeing those familiar faces. I could recognize almost all of them by name, career path, and domain, and slowly this feeling of familiarity was born.

This year I decided to invest in this event and attend it in person (supported and almost forced by [Tornike](https://www.linkedin.com/in/tornikeo/)). It was not a small decision, given that I had to take care of a lot of things out of my own pocket, but spoiler - it was all worth it!

Here are some highlights, conversations, and impressions from the conference that I just could not keep to myself, right?

## April 13

{% include carousel.html items=page.community_gathering label="Conference Venue" %}

The conference kicked off with a beautiful community night gathering at a local bar. We enjoyed some drinks and had warm conversations in the amazing, unofficial setting. This really helped break the ice and bring people together. I would highly recommend attending these kinds of icebreaker events if you ever get a chance. Who knows, you might even say hi to those you look up to 😏

On that night I finally met wonderful people in person from Switzerland ([Simon](https://www.linkedin.com/in/simonmartinelli), [Patrick](https://www.linkedin.com/in/patbaumgartner)), Germany ([Philip](https://www.linkedin.com/in/rieckpil/)), Lithuania ([Ina](https://www.linkedin.com/in/inabumstein)), Austria ([Philipp](https://www.linkedin.com/in/philipp-dangl), [Dusan](https://www.linkedin.com/in/du%C5%A1an-dom%C3%A1ny-a54a4a8), [Denys](https://www.linkedin.com/in/denysnahornyi)...), Norway (Anil), and many many more. I had a wonderful time discussing the future of AI and humanity alongside it (because who can avoid that at a tech conference?). These people stayed as anchors for me throughout the whole conference, helping me navigate the situation and enjoy my time there.

## April 14

{% include carousel.html items=page.conference_venue label="Conference Venue" %}

Conference venue was really really beautiful and well-chosen, with a magnificent garden around it.

{% include carousel.html items=page.interior label="Conference Venue Interior" %}

The interior was well-designed and comfortable, with huge halls that created a sense of freedom and creativity and naturally encouraged conversations.

Started the day with coffee and reunited with some of the colleagues from the previous night.

The opening ceremony included an amazing performance of Rosalia's Berghain (spot-on), and no one ever felt bored under the supervision of the wonderful host [Sanderson Jones](https://www.linkedin.com/in/sanderson-jones-2825b543).

Then came the sessions, and April 14 was the day when my brain was being pleasantly attacked from all directions.

{% include carousel.html items=page.sessions_14 label="April 14 Sessions" %}

Josh Long's **Bootiful Spring Boot 4** was exactly what you would expect from Josh: energetic, slightly chaotic in the best way, and packed with "wait, that's actually very useful" moments. A few things that stayed with me were API versioning becoming a first-class citizen, resilience-oriented additions like `@Retryable` and `@ConcurrencyLimit`, and the amount of work going into modern auth flows: passkeys, one-time tokens, and multi-factor support that feels much more natural than before. Also, the security customization story being more additive than destructive made me disproportionately happy. I always appreciate when a framework gives us more power without making us rewrite the world.

Later, **The Spring AI Ecosystem in 2026** by Mark Pollack, Christian Tzolov, and Dariusz Jedrzejczyk made one thing very obvious: the conversation has moved far beyond "how do I call an LLM from Java?" The interesting part now is orchestration. Advisors, memory, validation loops, structured output, tool discovery, skills, subagents - all of that felt like the real shape of agentic systems. One detail I especially liked was the emphasis on context optimization. This is probably one of the least glamorous but most important parts of building anything AI-related that has to survive real usage and not just a shiny demo.

Patrick Baumgartner's **From chaos to cohesion** was another highlight for me, probably because this topic scratches exactly the part of my brain that gets personally offended by messy codebases. His point that many companies pay the microservices tax too early really landed for me. The framing around vertical slicing was simple and strong: organize software the way people actually search for meaning, by module and responsibility, not by random technical categories. Spring Modulith came across as a very pragmatic tool here, not as a silver bullet, but as something that helps you introduce structure without signing up for distributed pain too early.

And then there was **Koog + Spring AI + Spring Boot**, which was one of those talks that makes you immediately want to sit down and play with ideas. The human physiology analogy was impossible not to remember: Spring Boot as the environment, Spring AI as the nerves, Koog as the brain, memory as memory, persistence as the immune system, observability as the mouth, history compression as sleep. Ridiculous? Maybe. Effective? Absolutely. More importantly, it presented a practical vision of agents in production: model switching, checkpointing, context management, and workflows that can survive the real world instead of collapsing the moment something goes slightly off script.

I also really enjoyed Simon Martinelli's **Spec-driven development** talk, mostly because it pushed back against the lazier side of AI enthusiasm. The core message was refreshingly sober: AI did change a lot, but not the need for clear requirements and design intent. If anything, it made those even more important. We can generate diagrams, code, and tests faster now, sure, but if the specification is weak, all we are doing is generating confusion at an impressive speed.


## April 15

Not bragging at all, but this was a special day as I took part in the community run event organized by IntelliJ itself (not kidding) - I even got the cutest limited running apparel. I was really stressed beforehand, fearing that I might have embarrassed myself and be crawling the whole time (it was 5-6 km, sweet spot for me, but "easy" by others' assessments). 

{% include carousel.html items=page.intellij_run label="Intellij Community Run" %}

We caught the early sunrise, ran through the area around the conference venue and started the day energized. It was the first time I had run in a group, and my running buddy (we were mostly running in pairs or a few people per line) Mauricio Salatino was perfect company, so I almost did not feel any fatigue at all. After a quick shower back at the hotel, the day continued with more interesting sessions.

{% include carousel.html items=page.sessions_15 label="April 15 Sessions" %}

Then came Mark Pollack's **From completions to goals**, which felt like a natural continuation of the broader Spring AI discussions from the previous day. Mark Pollack delivered a very helpful workshop that, in my humble interpretation, reminded everyone that vibe coding is not a sin, but trusting it blindly definitely is. One point I wrote down immediately was that we still do not have a good instinct for measuring agentic systems: did it actually do the task, how did it do it, and how do we know the behavior was actually good? The answer, again, was harnesses, feedback loops, and judgment. The line that stayed with me was essentially this: do everything in a deterministic way until you can't. Very good advice not only for AI, but for engineering in general.

One note I had in all caps was basically: *agent feedback is key*. If we cannot evaluate whether the agent did something well, then we are just vibing professionally. The workshop reinforced the same pattern I kept seeing across the conference: strong tooling around AI is less about "make the model smarter" and more about building safer loops, clearer evaluation, and more deliberate control.

Another session that caught my attention was Thomas Vitale's **Arconia for Spring Boot**. It appealed to the exact part of me that gets irritated by repetitive setup and project boilerplate. The promise was simple: fewer manually assembled development environments, fewer tiny workflow annoyances, more focus on building the actual application. Anything that reduces unnecessary friction without adding a new religion to the stack is already interesting to me.

Victor Rentea's **Top 10 event-driven architecture pitfalls** was also one of those talks that probably made a few people laugh nervously because the pain was too familiar. Event-driven systems are one of those things that sound elegant until duplicate deliveries, out-of-order events, race conditions, and lost messages enter the chat. Talks like this are always valuable because they cut through the architectural romance and remind us that distributed systems still collect blood as payment.

And finally, the observability session by Jonatan Ivanov and Tommy Ludwig was a strong reminder that it is not enough for applications to run; they need to be understandable while running. I always appreciate sessions that demystify things people love to make sound mystical. Logs, metrics, traces, health checks - these are not optional decorations for production systems, and every time someone explains them without unnecessary ceremony, I feel grateful.

***

{% include carousel.html items=page.selfies label="Happy selfies with the speakers" %}

I hope you can probably tell how much fun I had. But beyond fun, it was also a very important opportunity to shake hands with the leading experts in the field, meet a lot of people - experienced or beginners - from all around Europe, and confirm something I probably knew already: it is always worth stepping a little outside your comfort zone for the right community.

For those two days, that place felt very cozy and comfortable, almost like home - full of inspiring conversations and discussions buzzing around. A lot of people asked me if it was worth attending, and my answer is absolutely yes. And if it was worth overcoming the embarrassment and talking to completely unfamiliar people - more than absolutely, because that is exactly how you meet new friends, future collaborators, and the kind of people who make a community feel real.

{% include carousel.html items=page.fun_times label="Fun Times" %}

My absolute favorite moment was when our DJ Giuseppe, seeing that I was fangirling around the van and vibing to the great music, kindly offered to let me climb up there. It was the best!

Next year Spring I/O is set to be held in Valencia for the first time ever. Fingers crossed, you will see a new version of this diary from me around that time.
